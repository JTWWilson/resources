    The Tipitaka

    The TipitakaPali Canon - main Buddhist holy text
    is the collection of writings that make up the core of Buddhist holy texts. They are over 2000 years old but written a few hundred years after in Pali, the language spoken by the Buddha. The name Tipitaka comes from '3 baskets' which is how the original documents - written on palm leaves - were stored. The three baskets are: **vinaya pitaka**, **sutta pitaka** and **abhidhamma pitaka**.

    Buddhist scriptures as a whole are incredibly long and as so are not read by many Buddhists but they are taught by monks who chant sections regularly. In some traditions, copies of the texts are treated with great respect, sometimes placed on shrines. Despite this, critical thinking towards the books is encouraged and the Buddha’s mortality is not disguised.

    Ultimately, scriptures do not have intrinsic value, only, instrumental value - Dominique Side

    Vinaya

    • Contains many rules for monks and nuns.
    • Rules are elaborated on by stories from the Buddha’s life.
    • Written in response to disharmony in the
      SanghaOne of the three refuges - the Buddhist community
      .
    • Provides discipline in the Buddhist community.

    The Vinaya was the quality control system devised by the Buddha to maintain the purity of the Sangha - M. Meghaprasara

    Sutta

    • Means ‘the discourses’.
    • Contains more than 10,000 teachings from the Buddha or key disciples.
    • It is split up into 5 nikayas, or collections.

    Abhidhamma

    • Philosophical analysis of the Buddha’s teachings, mainly used in the Theravada tradition.
    • It considers the human psychology and main principles behind the Suttas and Vinayas
    • Written at least some decades after the others.
    • Intended to be read by an advanced monastic community.
    • M. Meghaprasara says that there is little new content just further exploration of the other books.
